Designing to Promote Equity and Change Authority

  • FCC

    Internet service providers would like us to believe that innovation at the physical layer of the OSI model is just as important. The physical layer, ISP’s argue, needs less institutional support and more freedom from unsavory regulation.

  • Business models

    ISP business models seek to manipulate both bandwidth speeds and network access. There is nothing truly innovative about providing packaged services with reduced service at a lower cost (as practiced in some parts of Europe). But this is a profit-friendly way to maximize (and racket) demand.

  • Politics

    Profit margins for an ISP really depend on clever handling of heavy network requests and limited oversight helps them meet this objective. When regulation directly impacts this very special layer of the OSI model (the physical layer) ISP’s often react with full force – deploying their lobbies and campaigning for their special interests.

  • ISP Goals

    The profits generated from the consumer, ISP’s promise, would drive corporate investment into new technologies, infrastructure, and innovation. The OSI layer closest to ISP business pursuits, the physical layer, is the part ISP’s control. Maintaining market capitalization in this area is both advantageous to their long-term sustainability and valuable to their short-term goals.

  • Naughty santa

    Most of net-neutrality attention is focused on theoretical abuses by internet service providers forgetting that much of the risk lies in the application layer. Sometimes innovation is filled with interesting examples of abuse, misconduct, and illegality. Other times innovation is fueled by exploitation, theft, and large-scale racketeering.

Proposed solution

My solution to this problem is a proposal to treat the physical layer of the OSI model as a public resource.

How It Works

  • Step 1 — Raise awareness

    Industry, academia can work together to promote strong electoral support.

  • Step 2 — Develop technical alternatives

    See what works and what doesn't. Many parts of the country are already experimenting with locally based municipal internet.

  • Step 3 — Tweak your ideas

    Communicate with end-users about change.

  • Step 4 — Look into other parts of the OSI layer

    Develop new approaches for the data-link, transport, and network layers. Seamless integration with IOT devices might require some sleight of hand and departure from the OSI model might be appropriate.
